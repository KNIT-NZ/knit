Our public institutions are propped up by operational systems that are painfully deprecated or bloated beyond understanding. Every public servant has felt their own private despair at entrenched models of work that create confusion, friction, or mediocrity.

At the same time, reforms that occupy space in public conversations are often sweeping. We hear about electoral finance reform, shiny new Ministerial portfolios, Government term extension, more Members of Parliament, Official Information Act shake-ups,, and other noble topics.

Executive-driven reforms require an exorbitant coincidence of wants. A tight legislative agenda and unrelenting constituency and media pressure leave no Cabinet bandwidth for new visions of governance. Even if this was not the case, top-down changes do little to address the invisible, systemic dysfunction known only to insiders.<FootnoteRef id="1" />

There is a version of change that does not wait for invitation. We prove this in our own lives, switching and shifting the objects and actors of our private spaces in response to innovation, competition, and inspiration.

Those who act on our behalf inside agencies and institutions can be given new tools that require no persuasion to adopt, tools conceived with sympathy for their incentives and limitations. One day, the right tools could also change the very nature of the relationship between those institutions and the citizens they serve.

We argue that open digital information systems can reinvigorate the operational models of public administration and at the same time move the needle on the strength of our democracy, and that we can begin now.
