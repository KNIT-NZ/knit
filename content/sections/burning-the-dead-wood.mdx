The bodies of law, regulation, and procedure that govern our lives are forms of hyperlinked text, where each part of the system is connected to and dependent on many others. But we have not given our system the affordances of hyperlinked text, which the internet has spent the past thirty years teaching us to navigate and order and imagine. Instead, we leave it to opaque and lumbering bureaucracies to steward the frameworks that shape our lives.

Reform is most often aimed at the substance of policy, and even when our best minds turn to governance we hear about sweeping changes like electoral finance reform, shiny new Ministerial portfolios, Government term extension, more Members of Parliament, Official Information Act shake-ups, and other noble topics. Executive-driven reforms require an exorbitant coincidence of wants and may mean almost nothing to most people.

We are planning instead to reshape the substrate of policy, the underlying base on which our laws and regulations are cultivated. There is a version of change that does not wait for government sanction. We prove this in our own lives, switching and shifting the objects and actors of our private spaces in response to innovation, competition, and inspiration.

We argue that open digital information systems can reinvigorate the operational models of public administration and at the same time move the needle on the strength of our democracy, and that we can begin now.
