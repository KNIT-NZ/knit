Profound changes to reality are rarely planned. Complex systems respond in novel ways to the peripheral and the unprecedented.

Conversely, dysfunctional systems do not become so by design, but by myriad innocuous decisions that build into an idol ripe for reflexive defense and justification.

Technologies presently within reach can unravel some of the managerial bloat and empower the innovators. Dry operational changes can also begin a cascade of radical transparency that spills out to the public.

Social contracts are fractured. The formidable challenges before us require diverse voices, the devolution of decision-making, and honest, reflective leadership. But saying a thing does not make it so, and while it is clear that many movements, thought leaders, and victims of the status quo are hungry for change, they cannot articulate a specific, scalable, and apolitical plan to catalyse it.
